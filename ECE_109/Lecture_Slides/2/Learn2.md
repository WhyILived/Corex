# Learn2: Photoelectric Effect and Photon Concept
> Estimated time: ~30 minutes

## Section 1: The Photoelectric Effect
- The photoelectric effect occurs when light shines on a metal surface and electrons are emitted
- Key observation: electron kinetic energy depends on light frequency (color), not intensity
- Increasing light intensity increases the number of electrons emitted (current), but not their energy
- Threshold frequency $f_0$ exists below which no photoemission occurs regardless of intensity

## Section 2: Einstein's Photon Theory
- Light consists of discrete energy packets called photons
- Each photon carries energy $E = hf$ where $h$ is Planck's constant
- When a photon strikes an electron, all its energy is transferred to that electron
- Electrons in a metal have potential energy lower by the work function $\Phi$ (work needed to escape)
- Maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons: $KE_{max} = hf - \Phi = hf - hf_0$
- Photoemission only occurs when $hf > \Phi$ (i.e., $f > f_0$)

## Section 3: Experimental Verification
- Millikan (1915) verified Einstein's equation experimentally
- The slope $h$ is universal (same for all metals)
- The threshold frequency $f_0$ (and thus $\Phi$) depends on the metal

## Key Equations
$$E_{photon} = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

Where:
- $E_{photon}$ = photon energy (J)
- $h$ = Planck's constant ($6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s)
- $f$ = frequency of light (Hz)
- $c$ = speed of light ($3 \times 10^8$ m/s)
- $\lambda$ = wavelength (m)

$$KE_{max} = hf - \Phi = eV_0$$

Where:
- $KE_{max}$ = maximum kinetic energy of emitted electron (J)
- $\Phi$ = work function of the metal (J or eV)
- $f$ = frequency of incident light (Hz)
- $e$ = electron charge ($1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C)
- $V_0$ = stopping voltage (V)

$$\Phi = hf_0$$

Where:
- $f_0$ = threshold frequency (Hz)

## Example
For blue light at 450 nm:
$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.6 \times 10^{-34})(3 \times 10^8)}{450 \times 10^{-9}} = 4.4 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} = 2.75 \text{ eV}$$
