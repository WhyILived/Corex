# Test4: De Broglie Relations - Electron as a Wave
> Tests: Learn4 material
> Time: ~15 minutes

## Conceptual Questions
1. State the de Broglie relationship and explain its physical significance.
2. Why do we not observe wave-like behavior (diffraction) with everyday objects like a tennis ball?
3. What is Bragg's law and why is it important for electron diffraction?
4. Why is electron diffraction useful for studying crystal structures?

## Calculation Problems
1. An electron is accelerated through a potential difference of 1 kV. Calculate its de Broglie wavelength.
   - Answer: $\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e eV}} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{\sqrt{2(9.11 \times 10^{-31})(1.6 \times 10^{-19})(1000)}} = 0.0388$ nm

2. An electron has a de Broglie wavelength of 0.10 nm.
   - (a) Calculate its momentum.
   - (b) Calculate its kinetic energy in electron-volts.
   - Answers: (a) $p = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{0.1 \times 10^{-9}} = 6.63 \times 10^{-24}$ kg·m/s; (b) $KE = \frac{p^2}{2m_e} = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-24})^2}{2(9.11 \times 10^{-31})} = 2.41 \times 10^{-17}$ J $= 151$ eV

3. Compare the de Broglie wavelength of an electron (50 V acceleration) to that of a proton moving at 1000 m/s.
   - Electron (50 V): $\lambda_e \approx 0.174$ nm
   - Proton: $\lambda_p = \frac{h}{mv} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{(1.67 \times 10^{-27})(1000)} = 3.97 \times 10^{-10}$ m
   - Answer: The proton wavelength is about $4 \times 10^{-10}$ m, much longer than the electron's because the proton is much more massive.

## Answers
1. $\lambda = h/p$ or $p = h/\lambda$. It states that every particle has an associated wavelength inversely proportional to its momentum. This unified the wave-particle duality: light has particle properties (photons with $E = hf, p = h/\lambda$) and matter has wave properties.
2. Macroscopic objects have large masses, so their de Broglie wavelengths are extremely small (e.g., a tennis ball has $\lambda \sim 10^{-34}$ m). Wave effects require wavelength comparable to the feature size, so no observable diffraction occurs.
3. Bragg's law: $2d\sin\theta = n\lambda$ gives the condition for constructive interference from crystal planes separated by distance $d$. It explains the diffraction pattern and allows calculation of crystal structure from diffraction measurements.
4. Electrons accelerated by typical voltages (50-100 V) have wavelengths (~0.1 nm) comparable to interatomic spacings in solids. This allows electron diffraction to probe crystal structures, similar to X-ray diffraction but with the advantage that electron beams can be focused and deflected.
