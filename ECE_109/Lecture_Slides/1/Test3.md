## Conceptual Questions

1. State the three key observations of the photoelectric effect that could not be explained by classical wave theory of light.

2. According to Einstein's explanation of the photoelectric effect, what is a photon? How does the photon energy depend on the light's frequency?

3. In the photoelectric effect equation $KE_{max} = hf - \Phi$, identify what each term represents and explain the physical meaning of the work function.

4. Two different metals are illuminated with light of the same wavelength. Metal A has a higher work function than Metal B. Which metal will emit electrons with greater maximum kinetic energy? Explain using the photoelectric equation.

5. Light intensity and photon energy are both properties of light. How are they related to each other through photon flux density?

## Calculation Problems

1. Calculate the energy of a photon with wavelength $\lambda = 500$ nm. Express your answer in both Joules and electron-volts (eV).
   - $h = 6.6 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s
   - $1$ eV $= 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ J

2. The work function of sodium is $\Phi = 1.9$ eV.
   - Convert this to Joules.
   - What is the maximum wavelength of light that can cause photoelectron emission from sodium?
   - If light with wavelength 400 nm shines on sodium, what is the maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons?

3. A beam of light has intensity $I = 100$ W/m² and wavelength $\lambda = 600$ nm. Calculate:
   - The photon energy in joules
   - The photon flux density $\Gamma_{ph}$ (photons per m² per second)

4. In a photoelectric experiment, electrons are stopped by a potential $V_0 = 2.0$ V when light of frequency $f = 1.0 \times 10^{15}$ Hz shines on a metal with work function $\Phi = 2.0$ eV.
   - Calculate the kinetic energy of the fastest electrons in Joules.
   - Verify that the photoelectric equation is satisfied by calculating $hf - \Phi$.

## Answers

1.
   - **Observation 1**: Electrons are emitted only when light frequency exceeds a threshold (depends on metal). Classical theory predicted intensity, not frequency, should determine electron energy.
   - **Observation 2**: The kinetic energy of emitted electrons increases with light frequency (shorter wavelength), not with intensity. Classical theory predicted higher intensity = higher electron energy.
   - **Observation 3**: The number of electrons emitted depends on light intensity, but their energy depends on frequency. Classical theory predicted both should depend on intensity.

2. A photon is a particle (quantized packet) of light. According to Einstein, the energy of each photon is proportional to its frequency: $E_{photon} = hf = \hbar\omega$. Higher frequency light (blue/violet) has more energetic photons, while lower frequency light (red/infrared) has less energetic photons.

3. - $KE_{max}$ = maximum kinetic energy of emitted electron (J or eV)
   - $h$ = Planck's constant ($6.6 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s)
   - $f$ = frequency of incident light (Hz)
   - $\Phi$ = work function of the metal — the minimum energy needed to liberate an electron from the metal surface

   The work function is a property of the metal and represents the energy binding the electron to the metal.

4. Metal B will emit electrons with greater kinetic energy. From the equation $KE_{max} = hf - \Phi$, for the same $f$ (and thus same $hf$), a smaller $\Phi$ means the remaining energy goes into electron kinetic energy. Since Metal A has a higher work function, fewer electrons will also be emitted (assuming $hf > \Phi$ for both).

5. Light intensity $I$ is the energy flowing per unit area per second. Photon flux density $\Gamma_{ph}$ is the number of photons arriving per unit area per second. The relationship is: $I = \Gamma_{ph} \times E_{photon} = \Gamma_{ph} \times hf$. So intensity depends on both how many photons arrive and how energetic each photon is.

**Calculation Answers:**

1. $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.6 \times 10^{-34})(3 \times 10^8)}{500 \times 10^{-9}} = 3.96 \times 10^{-19}$ J
   In eV: $E = \frac{3.96 \times 10^{-19}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 2.48$ eV

2.
   - $\Phi = 1.9$ eV $\times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ J/eV $= 3.04 \times 10^{-19}$ J
   - Maximum wavelength: $hf = \Phi$, so $\lambda_{max} = \frac{hc}{\Phi} = \frac{(6.6 \times 10^{-34})(3 \times 10^8)}{3.04 \times 10^{-19}} = 651$ nm
   - For $\lambda = 400$ nm: $KE_{max} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} - \Phi = \frac{(6.6 \times 10^{-34})(3 \times 10^8)}{400 \times 10^{-9}} - 3.04 \times 10^{-19} = 4.95 \times 10^{-19} - 3.04 \times 10^{-19} = 1.91 \times 10^{-19}$ J $= 1.19$ eV

3.
   - Photon energy: $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.6 \times 10^{-34})(3 \times 10^8)}{600 \times 10^{-9}} = 3.3 \times 10^{-19}$ J
   - Photon flux density: $\Gamma_{ph} = \frac{I}{E} = \frac{100}{3.3 \times 10^{-19}} = 3.03 \times 10^{20}$ photons/m²·s

4.
   - $KE_{max} = eV_0 = (1.6 \times 10^{-19})(2.0) = 3.2 \times 10^{-19}$ J $= 2.0$ eV
   - $hf = (6.6 \times 10^{-34})(1.0 \times 10^{15}) = 6.6 \times 10^{-19}$ J $= 4.13$ eV
   - $hf - \Phi = 4.13$ eV $- 2.0$ eV $= 2.13$ eV $= 3.41 \times 10^{-19}$ J

   Note: There is a slight discrepancy ($2.0$ vs $2.13$ eV), which could be due to rounding or experimental considerations.
