# Learn5: Stimulated Emission and Lasers
> Estimated time: ~30 minutes

## Section 1: Three Key Processes
### Absorption
An electron at lower energy $E_1$ absorbs a photon of energy $hf = E_2 - E_1$ and moves to higher energy $E_2$.

### Spontaneous Emission
An electron at higher energy $E_2$ spontaneously decays to lower energy $E_1$, emitting a photon of energy $hf = E_2 - E_1$. This is a random process.

### Stimulated Emission
An incoming photon with energy $hf = E_2 - E_1$ stimulates an electron at $E_2$ to decay to $E_1$, emitting a second photon. The emitted photon is:
- **Identical in energy** (same frequency)
- **In phase** (coherent)
- **Traveling in same direction** as the stimulating photon

This creates **two identical photons** from one - the basis for light amplification!

## Section 2: Population Inversion
For light amplification, we need more atoms in excited state $E_2$ than ground state $E_1$ - this is **population inversion**.

With two energy levels alone, population inversion is impossible in steady state.

Solution: Use a **three-level system** (or four-level) with a **metastable state**.

## Section 3: The Laser Principle
LASER = Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation

Requirements for laser action:
1. **Population inversion** - more atoms in excited state
2. **Optical cavity** - mirrors at ends to reflect photons back and forth
3. **Metastable state** - long-lived upper state to maintain inversion

## Section 4: Ruby Laser
First laser (1960) - Theodore Maiman

Structure:
- Ruby rod (Al₂O₃:Cr³⁺) with mirrored ends
- Xenon flash lamp for pumping

Energy levels (Cr³⁺ ion):
- $E_1$: Ground state
- $E_3$: Pump level (via flash)
- $E_2$: Metastable state (long-lived upper level)

Process:
1. Pumping: Electrons excited to $E_3$ by xenon flash
2. Fast decay: $E_3 \to E_2$ (metastable) + lattice vibrations
3. Population accumulates at $E_2$
4. One spontaneous emission triggers avalanche of stimulated emissions
5. Coherent photons bounce between mirrors, amplifying through stimulated emission
6. Partial transmission at one mirror = laser output

Output: 694.3 nm (red pulse)

## Section 5: Helium-Neon Laser
Continuous wave (CW) laser - Ali Javan (1960)

Structure:
- He-Ne gas mixture in discharge tube
- Mirrored cavity ends

Process:
1. Electron collision excites He to He* (metastable)
2. Resonance energy transfer: He* + Ne $\to$ He + Ne*
3. Population inversion in Ne between 5s and 3p levels
4. Stimulated emission at 632.8 nm (red)

Efficiency: Very low (~0.025%)

## Section 6: Laser Properties
Lasers produce light that is:
- **Monochromatic**: Single wavelength (or very narrow range)
- **Coherent**: All photons in phase
- **Collimated**: Low divergence beam
- **High intensity**: Can focus enormous power

## Key Equations
Stimulated emission rate depends on photon flux and Einstein B coefficient.

## Key Definitions
- **Absorption**: Process where photon energy raises electron to higher level
- **Spontaneous emission**: Random photon emission from excited state
- **Stimulated emission**: Photon-induced emission, producing coherent photon
- **Population inversion**: More atoms in excited state than ground state
- **Metastable state**: Excited state with unusually long lifetime
- **Optical cavity**: Mirror arrangement to amplify light through multiple passes
- **Coherent photons**: Photons with same phase, frequency, and direction
