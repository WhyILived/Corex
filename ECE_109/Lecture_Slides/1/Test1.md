# Test1: Knowledge Check - Light and Wave-Particle Duality
> Tests: Learn1 material
> Time: ~15 minutes

## Conceptual Questions

1. State the difference between constructive and destructive interference in Young's double-slit experiment. Under what condition does each occur?

2. Explain why the photoelectric effect demonstrates the particle-like nature of light. What would happen if we only used the wave model of light to explain this effect?

3. What is meant by "wave-particle duality" of light? Give one example where light behaves as a wave and one where it behaves as a particle.

4. Define the work function of a metal. How is it related to the threshold frequency in the photoelectric effect?

5. Briefly explain how our understanding of light evolved from Newton's particle theory to Maxwell's wave theory to Planck/Einstein's quantum theory.

6. What is the physical meaning of the wave equation $\nabla^2u - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}u = 0$? Why is it important?

7. Distinguish between gradient, divergence, and curl operations. Give one physical example where each would be used.

## Calculation Problems

1. Calculate the energy of a photon with wavelength 450 nm (blue light). Express your answer in both Joules and electron-volts (eV).

2. In a photoelectric experiment, light of wavelength 522 nm is incident on a clean sodium surface. If the work function of sodium is 2.38 eV, what is the kinetic energy of the emitted electrons in eV?

3. X-rays with wavelength 0.6 Å (angstroms) are used in mammography. Calculate:
   a) The energy of these X-ray photons in keV
   b) The momentum of these photons in kg·m/s

4. Light with intensity 20 mW/cm² and wavelength 250 nm is incident on a sodium surface. If each photon releases one electron, calculate the photoelectric current density in mA/cm².

## Answers

1. 
   - Constructive interference: path difference = nλ (n = 0, 1, 2, ...); waves add together
   - Destructive interference: path difference = (n + 1/2)λ; waves cancel out

2. The photoelectric effect shows that:
   - Electron energy depends only on light frequency, not intensity (contradicts wave model where more intense light should give more energy)
   - There's a threshold frequency below which no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity
   - This supports the photon (particle) model where energy is quantized per photon

3. Wave-particle duality means light exhibits both wave and particle properties depending on the experiment:
   - Wave behavior: Young's double-slit experiment, X-ray diffraction
   - Particle behavior: Photoelectric effect, Compton scattering

4. The work function Φ is the minimum energy needed to release an electron from a metal's surface. It relates to threshold frequency by: Φ = hf₀, so f₀ = Φ/h

**Calculation Answers**:

1. $E = hc/\lambda = (6.6 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8)/(450 \times 10^{-9}) = 4.4 \times 10^{-19}$ J = 2.75 eV

2. $KE = hc/(e\lambda) - \Phi = (6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8)/(1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 522 \times 10^{-9}) - 2.38 = 4.97 - 2.38 = 2.59$ eV

3. 
   a) $E = hc/(\lambda \times 1.6 \times 10^{-16}) = (6.6 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8)/(0.6 \times 10^{-10} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}) = 20.6$ keV
   b) $p = h/\lambda = 6.6 \times 10^{-34}/(0.6 \times 10^{-10}) = 1.1 \times 10^{-23}$ kg·m/s

4. $J = eI\lambda/(hc) = (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 20 \times 10^{-3} \times 10^4 \times 250 \times 10^{-9})/(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8) = 4.0$ mA/cm²
