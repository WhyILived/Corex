## Section 1: Light as a Wave - Young's Double-Slit Experiment

Light exhibits wave behavior through interference, demonstrated by Young's double-slit experiment.

When monochromatic light passes through two slits (S1 and S2), the resulting wave patterns interfere constructively and destructively on a screen.

### Electric Field Equations
The electric field from each slit at point P:

$$\vec{E}_1(\vec{r}, t) = \vec{E}_0 \cos(\vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}_1 - \omega t + \phi_1)$$

$$\vec{E}_2(\vec{r}, t) = \vec{E}_0 \cos(\vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}_2 - \omega t + \phi_2)$$

### Intensity Pattern
The combined intensity at point P:

$$I = \frac{1}{2}c\epsilon_0|\vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2|^2$$

### Interference Conditions
- **Constructive interference (bright fringes)**: $S1P - S2P = n\lambda$ where $n = 0, 1, 2, ...$
- **Destructive interference (dark fringes)**: $S1P - S2P = (n + \frac{1}{2})\lambda$

### Key Insight
$$|cos(x) + cos(y)|^2 \neq |cos(x)|^2 + |cos(y)|^2$$

This demonstrates that interference is not simply additive—the phase relationship between waves matters.

## Section 2: Light as Particles - Photon Properties

Light also behaves as particles (photons), with quantized energy and momentum.

### Photon Energy
$$E_{photon} = hf = h\nu = \hbar\omega$$

Where:
- $E_{photon}$ = photon energy (J)
- $h$ = Planck's constant ($6.6 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s)
- $\nu$ = frequency (Hz)
- $\hbar$ = reduced Planck's constant ($\hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi} = 1.05 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s)
- $\omega$ = angular frequency (rad/s)

### Photon Momentum
Despite having zero mass, photons carry momentum:

$$p = \frac{E_{photon}}{c} = \frac{hf}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}$$

Where:
- $p$ = photon momentum (kg·m/s)
- $c$ = speed of light ($3 \times 10^8$ m/s)
- $\lambda$ = wavelength (m)

### Light Intensity
Light intensity (power per unit area) relates to the electric field amplitude:

$$I = \frac{1}{2}c\epsilon_0 E_0^2$$

The intensity can also be expressed in terms of photon flux:

$$I = \Gamma_{ph} hf$$

Where $\Gamma_{ph} = \frac{\Delta N_{ph}}{A\Delta t}$ is the photon flux density (photons per unit area per second).

## Section 3: Applications of Photon Momentum

### Radiation Pressure
Photons transfer momentum to surfaces, creating radiation pressure:
- At Earth's orbit, perfect absorbance exerts $F = 4.54$ μN/m²
- Perfect reflectance exerts $F = 9.08$ μN/m²

### Solar Sails
Spacecraft can use radiation pressure from sunlight to propel vessels through space.

### Optical Tweezers
Focused laser beams can hold and manipulate microscopic objects because the sphere is pushed toward the high-intensity region due to momentum transfer of refracted photons.
