## Section 1: Light as Particles - The Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect demonstrates that light behaves as particles (photons). When light strikes a metal surface, electrons can be emitted.

### Key Experimental Observations

1. **Electron kinetic energy depends on wavelength (color/frequency)**: The maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons is determined by the light frequency, not intensity.

2. **Electron kinetic energy depends on metal type**: Different metals have different work functions.

3. **Electron kinetic energy does NOT depend on light intensity**: Doubling the light intensity emits more electrons but doesn't increase their energy.

4. **Threshold frequency**: Below a certain frequency (specific to each metal), no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity.

### The Photoelectric Equation

$$KE_{max} = eV_0 = \frac{1}{2}m_ev^2 = hf - \Phi_{metal}$$

Where:
- $KE_{max}$ = maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons (J)
- $e$ = elementary charge ($1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C)
- $V_0$ = stopping potential (V)
- $m_e$ = electron mass ($9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg)
- $v$ = electron velocity (m/s)
- $f$ = frequency of incident light (Hz)
- $\Phi_{metal}$ = work function of the metal (J)

### Work Function

The work function $\Phi_{metal}$ is the minimum energy needed to liberate an electron from the metal surface. Different metals have different work functions.

### Physical Interpretation

- A single photon transfers all its energy $hf$ to a single electron
- Part of this energy ($\Phi_{metal}$) is used to escape the metal
- The remaining energy becomes the electron's kinetic energy

### Important Note
Photoelectrons leave the surface with a distribution of energies, not all at $KE_{max}$.
