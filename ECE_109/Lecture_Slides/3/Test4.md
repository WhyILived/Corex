# Test4: De Broglie Waves and Electron Diffraction
> Tests: Learn4 material
> Time: ~15 minutes

## Conceptual Questions

1. State the de Broglie hypothesis in your own words. How does it extend the wave-particle duality concept?

2. Why don't we observe wave behavior (diffraction/interference) for macroscopic objects like baseballs?

3. In Young's double-slit experiment with electrons, what would you observe if you sent electrons through one slit at a time? Would the interference pattern still form?

4. An electron and a proton are both accelerated to the same velocity. Compare their de Broglie wavelengths. Which is larger?

## Calculation Problems

1. **Electron de Broglie Wavelength**
   
   Calculate the de Broglie wavelength of an electron moving at $v = 1.0 \times 10^6$ m/s.
   
   Given: $m_e = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg, $h = 6.6 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s
   
   Expected answer: $\lambda \approx 7.3 \times 10^{-10}$ m (about 0.73 nm)

2. **50 kV Electron Wavelength**
   
   Electrons are accelerated through a potential difference of 50,000 V. Calculate their de Broglie wavelength.
   
   Hint: First find the momentum using $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = eV$, then find $\lambda = h/p$.
   
   Given: $m_e = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg, $h = 6.6 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s, $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C
   
   Expected answer: $\lambda \approx 5.5 \times 10^{-12}$ m (about 0.0055 nm)

3. **Baseball Wavelength**
   
   A baseball (mass 0.15 kg) is thrown at $v = 40$ m/s. Calculate its de Broglie wavelength.
   
   Expected answer: $\lambda \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-34}$ m (extremely small, undetectable)

4. **Matching Wavelengths**
   
   Find the velocity at which an electron would have the same de Broglie wavelength as a photon with wavelength 550 nm (green light).
   
   Expected answer: $v \approx 1300$ m/s

## Answers

1. **Electron at $10^6$ m/s**:
   
   $p = m_e v = (9.11 \times 10^{-31})(1.0 \times 10^6) = 9.11 \times 10^{-25}$ kg·m/s
   
   $\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{6.6 \times 10^{-34}}{9.11 \times 10^{-25}} = 7.24 \times 10^{-10}$ m $\approx 7.3 \times 10^{-10}$ m

2. **50 kV electron**:
   
   $KE = eV = (1.6 \times 10^{-19})(50,000) = 8.0 \times 10^{-15}$ J
   
   Using non-relativistic approximation (50 keV is moderately relativistic but this gives good estimate):
   
   $p = \sqrt{2m_e KE} = \sqrt{2(9.11 \times 10^{-31})(8.0 \times 10^{-15})} = 3.82 \times 10^{-22}$ kg·m/s
   
   $\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{6.6 \times 10^{-34}}{3.82 \times 10^{-22}} = 1.73 \times 10^{-12}$ m $\approx 0.0055$ nm

3. **Baseball**:
   
   $p = mv = (0.15)(40) = 6.0$ kg·m/s
   
   $\lambda = \frac{6.6 \times 10^{-34}}{6.0} = 1.1 \times 10^{-34}$ m

4. **Matching 550 nm**:
   
   For photon: $p_{photon} = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{6.6 \times 10^{-34}}{550 \times 10^{-9}} = 1.2 \times 10^{-27}$ kg·m/s
   
   For electron at velocity $v$: $p_e = m_e v$
   
   Setting $p_e = p_{photon}$:
   
   $v = \frac{p_{photon}}{m_e} = \frac{1.2 \times 10^{-27}}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}} \approx 1300$ m/s
