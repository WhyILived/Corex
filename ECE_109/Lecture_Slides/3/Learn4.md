## Section 1: The Electron as a Wave

### de Broglie's Hypothesis
If light (waves) can behave as particles (photons), then perhaps particles (like electrons) can behave as waves. Louis de Broglie proposed this symmetry in 1924.

### de Broglie Relations
The wavelength of a particle is inversely proportional to its momentum:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} \quad \text{or} \quad p = \frac{h}{\lambda}$$

Where:
- $\lambda$ = wavelength (m)
- $p$ = momentum (kg·m/s)
- $h$ = Planck's constant ($6.6 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s)

These are the de Broglie relations, connecting wave properties ($\lambda$) to particle properties ($p$).

### Recall: Photon Momentum
This extends the photon momentum relation $p = h/\lambda$ to all particles with momentum.

## Section 2: Experimental Verification - Electron Diffraction

### Young's Double-Slit with Electrons
When electrons pass through two slits (like in Young's original experiment with light), they produce interference fringes on a fluorescent screen.

The experimental setup:
- Electron gun (filament at 50 kV)
- Two slits
- Fluorescent screen in vacuum (CRT-like structure)
- Observed: diffraction fringes identical in pattern to light interference

### What This Means
The observation of interference patterns from electrons confirms that particles exhibit wave behavior. This is direct experimental evidence for the de Broglie hypothesis.

## Section 3: Matter Waves Significance

### Wave-Particle Duality Extended
The duality between waves and particles applies to all matter—not just light. Both photons and electrons (and indeed all particles) exhibit both wave and particle properties.

### Characteristic Scales
For macroscopic objects, the de Broglie wavelength is immeasurably small, which is why we don't observe wave behavior in everyday life.

Example: An electron accelerated through 50 kV has a wavelength comparable to atomic spacings, which is why electron diffraction is useful for studying crystal structures.
